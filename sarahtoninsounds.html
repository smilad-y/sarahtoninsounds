May 1, 2026 · essay
The Sound of a People: Why Music IS the Archive

There is a reason that enslaved people were forbidden from drumming. Not because drums were loud — though they were — but because the people who controlled them understood something we have systematically failed to teach: music carries information that cannot be written down, and cannot be taken.

Rhythm is memory. Melody is migration. Harmony is the record of who came together and how. When you hear a song, you're not just hearing sound — you're hearing the accumulated choices of a people about what mattered, what hurt, what was worth celebrating, and what was worth surviving for.

In the age of streaming, we've become very good at accessing music and very bad at understanding it. The algorithm serves you a track from the Niger Delta next to one from Oslo and calls both "chill vibes." Nothing is wrong with either song. Everything is wrong with the framework.

The archive isn't just academic. It's alive. It lives in the musicians who still know the old ways, in the grandmothers who hum songs they never learned the words to, in the communities who carry something in their chests they can't quite name. When we lose the context, we lose the key.

This site is my small, chaotic, sincere attempt to push back against the flattening. To say: this song came from somewhere specific. These people made something. Here is why it matters.

What Gets Lost When Songs Can't Travel Anymore

Sync licensing was — in theory — one of the most democratic developments in music history. A song from a bedroom in Lagos could end up soundtracking a scene watched by 40 million people. That happened. It still happens. But the infrastructure built around it has introduced a new kind of filter that most people never see.

Rights. Clearances. Publisher relationships. PRO registrations. Without those, your song doesn't travel — not through film, not through TV, not through the cultural corridors that turn "regional artist" into "iconic sound." The artists who need exposure most are often the ones least equipped to navigate the system designed to give it to them.

I've spent years inside this system. It's not evil — it's just not neutral. And the gap between the music that deserves to be heard and the music that actually gets placed is not a gap in quality. It's a gap in access, in infrastructure, in who has a publisher with the right relationships.

I don't have a clean answer for this. I just want more people to see the system for what it is — so we can start asking better questions about how to change it.

Dopamine, Rhythm, and Why Some Songs Feel Like Home

There's a reason you can hear four bars of a song you haven't thought about in fifteen years and suddenly be completely somewhere else. Your body was there before your brain caught up. That's not nostalgia — that's neuroscience. Music encodes memory differently than almost any other stimulus, which is why it can unlock places in you that words and images can't reach.

Dopamine is part of it. Rhythm entrainment — the way your nervous system literally synchronizes with a beat — is part of it. But there's something else too: the way a particular sound can carry the emotional signature of a whole community, a whole era, a whole way of being in the world.

That's why the loss is more than aesthetic when a musical tradition disappears. You're not just losing songs. You're losing a technology of feeling — a shared language for processing grief and joy and everything in between that a specific group of people built, over time, together.

This is what I mean when I say music is the archive. The body knows before the mind does.

March 22, 2026 · essay
African Music and the Western Gaze: A Love Story Nobody Asked For

Every few years, Western media "discovers" a genre that has been thriving on its own terms for decades. Right now it's Amapiano. Before that, Afrobeats. The cycle is predictable: first the feature, then the festival booking, then the collab with the American artist who lends "credibility," then the Grammy nomination that frames a living tradition as an exotic novelty — and then, often, the quiet fading once the moment passes.

When a genre is discovered rather than encountered, the narrative belongs to the discoverer. The history drops away. The community drops away. What remains is a sound, stripped of its origins, available for use.

I love that Tems is on a world stage. I love that Burna Boy has a Grammy. But I also want to hold the tension: there is a way of loving a music that respects it, and a way that consumes it. The question isn't "is this music good?" The question is: who tells the story, and who gets to keep it?

music supervision ✦ what it is & why it matters

You've felt it before — a scene comes on screen and a song hits and everything opens up. That's not an accident. That's music supervision.

Music supervision is the art of finding the right song for the right moment — and the craft of making sure it can legally live there.
what it actually is

The Bridge Between Music and Storytelling

A music supervisor works at the intersection of creative vision and industry infrastructure. We source music for film, TV, advertising, and games — finding tracks that don't just fit a scene, but deepen it. The best placement doesn't call attention to itself. It makes you feel something you couldn't articulate why. It demands an enormous library in your head, genuine emotional intelligence, and a working knowledge of music law — all at once.

the art + craft

How Songs Find Their Scenes

1

Creative Brief

Understanding the story — the tone, the era, the character's emotional state, what the director wants the audience to feel.

2

Search & Discovery

Digging through catalogs, reaching out to publishers and indie artists, pulling from years of deep listening. This is where taste lives.

3

Clearance & Licensing

Every song has two copyrights — master and composition. Both need clearing. Negotiating with labels, publishers, and sometimes the artists themselves.

4

Cue Sheets & Administration

After the show airs, cue sheets are filed with PROs so that everyone who made the music actually gets paid.

why it matters culturally

Music Supervision as an Act of Preservation

Music supervisors are cultural gatekeepers, whether we like it or not. The songs placed in major productions are the ones preserved in cultural memory. A placement in a beloved show can resurrect a forgotten artist, introduce a genre to millions, or cement a song's place in the cultural record forever. Done thoughtfully, it's one of the most powerful tools we have for keeping musical cultures alive and in conversation with each other.

who is behind this ✦

Hi. I'm Sarah Milad — music industry professional, sync licensing obsessive, and founder of Sarahtonin Sounds and Music Soup.

I've spent years inside the machine: coordinating music for live broadcast television, clearing rights, building libraries, processing cue sheets at volumes that would make a librarian weep. I know how music travels through the industry. And that knowledge has made me increasingly preoccupied with what doesn't travel — and why.

The intersection of music and culture isn't a niche interest for me. It's the whole thing. Music is how communities encode identity, process trauma, celebrate survival, and pass down what matters. When we lose musical traditions, we lose archives that cannot be reconstructed.

This site is my corner of the internet to push back against the flattening. Based in Los Angeles. Guild of Music Supervisors member. UCLA Extension cert. in Music Supervision. Perpetually listening.
